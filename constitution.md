Yok is a reader of unusual depth and range, but he is not interested in breadth for its own sake—he wants to encounter ideas at the level of the practitioner, not the science journalist. The most reliable signal in his feedback is a frustration with accessibility: a 4-star article on formal proofs in mathematics was docked implicitly because it spoke to curious laypeople rather than to working mathematicians. He wants to understand *how the math actually works*, not just that it is interesting that it exists.

He responds to novelty more than confirmation. When a topic was already familiar (the central limit theorem and bell curves), he wanted more, not a restatement—"would have appreciated more novelty and depth." This suggests his baseline curiosity is high and that he dislikes being told things he already knows, even when they are framed entertainingly. The corollary is that articles should bring him into genuinely unfamiliar territory—unusual subfields, edge cases, surprising empirical findings, or conceptual moves that haven't been normalized in mainstream science writing.

His highest-rated piece was a demographic deep dive into South Korea's fertility collapse—a combination of unusual empirical subject matter with genuine analytical depth. The "fascinating" rating on the Fermi Paradox article and his multiple "looked very cool" notes on topics ranging from jellyfish circadian rhythms to robotic manipulation suggest that the actual subject matter matters less than the quality of the investigation. He is willing to follow writers anywhere if the writing is doing something intellectually serious.

He is skeptical of listicles and synthesis pieces. He rated a "26 best ideas" format as "too obvious"—synthesis formats typically trade depth for breadth, and he finds this trade unfavorable. Charts and empirical specificity are appreciated ("the charts were nice"), which signals quantitative instincts. But the numbers need to illuminate something non-obvious; they cannot serve as decoration.

His interest in AI and formal systems is genuine but nuanced. He wants the formally rigorous treatment: the philosopher of mathematics asking whether proof assistants are taking rigor too far, not the technology reporter covering "AI is changing how we prove theorems." Similarly with robotics: the question of *why* manipulation is fundamentally hard is more interesting to him than a company product launch narrative.

The rating scale he uses is 7-point (he notes 4.5/7, 4/7, 3/7), which maps loosely to the 5-star system. This granularity suggests he is a careful evaluator who distinguishes between strong, adequate, and exceptional rather than collapsing everything into the top tier.

In terms of sources, the quality of argument matters more than venue prestige. Works in Progress consistently delivers empirical depth on infrastructure, policy, history, and demographics—well aligned with his profile. Quanta Magazine is a mixed case: the writing can tilt toward consumer-accessible, but the underlying mathematics and science is usually substantive enough to earn its place. Nautilus is similar—more miss than hit, but occasionally produces genuinely fascinating pieces.

He does not show strong political commitments in his reading preferences. The demographic and policy pieces he engages with tend toward the analytically empirical rather than the ideologically motivated. Wildcards should genuinely challenge: non-Western epistemologies, religious or spiritual philosophy taken on its own terms, or contrarian takes on technology's promises that require engaging with the actual technical limitations rather than hand-waving them away. The wildcard is most successful when it's not merely "uncomfortable" but when it contains a real argument that could alter his view of a topic he thought he'd settled.
