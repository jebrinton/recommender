# Reading List — March 31, 2026

*Curated for expanding your worldview with quality, balanced content across science, philosophy, economics, and technology.*

---

## Big Ideas & Economics

**"The Marginal Revolution: Rise and Decline, and the Pending AI Revolution"** — Tyler Cowen
A free, four-chapter, ~40,000-word work examining the history of marginalism in economics and why it may dwindle as AI reshapes the discipline. Covers Jevons, the slow development of economic science, and what comes next. A substantial read for anyone interested in how foundational economic thinking is being disrupted.
→ [Read on Marginal Revolution](https://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2026/03/marginal-revolution-rise-and-decline-and-the-pending-ai-revolution.html)

**"The 26 Most Important Ideas for 2026"** — Derek Thompson
Includes "The Monks in the Casino," examining how young people (especially men) are spending unprecedented time alone gambling online, with the share of 20-24 year-olds not in jobs, school, or raising children nearly doubling in the last quarter-century. A sobering look at isolation and digital dependency.
→ [Read on Derek Thompson's Substack](https://www.derekthompson.org/p/the-26-most-important-ideas-for-2026)

**"What to Watch in 2026"** — Harvard Business School (multiple authors)
A collection of essays from Harvard experts on management and society. Highlights include the "AI Satisfaction Gap" — the finding that generative AI boosts quality and efficiency but paradoxically lowers user satisfaction — and other forecasts on business and societal shifts.
→ [Read at HBS](https://www.hbs.edu/bigs/bigs-what-to-watch-in-2026)

## Science & Mathematics

**"In Expanding de Sitter Space, Quantum Mechanics Gets Even More Elusive"** — Shalma Wegsman, *Quanta Magazine*
Our expanding universe's geometry creates deep puzzles for quantum mechanics. Physicists are finding that the shape of de Sitter space — which describes our cosmos — makes quantum phenomena harder to pin down than ever.
→ [Read on Quanta](https://www.quantamagazine.org/archive/) (March 30, 2026)

**"Are Strings Still Our Best Hope for a Theory of Everything?"** — Natalie Wolchover, *Quanta Magazine*
A fresh examination of string theory's status in 2026. As the debate over unification continues, this piece surveys the latest developments and whether strings remain the leading candidate for a final theory.
→ [Read on Quanta](https://www.quantamagazine.org/archive/) (March 23, 2026)

**"In Math, Rigor Is Vital. But Are Digitized Proofs Taking It Too Far?"** — Leila Sloman, *Quanta Magazine*
As mathematicians formalize proofs in the computer program Lean, a fascinating tension emerges: does machine-verified rigor enhance mathematics or constrain mathematical creativity? A timely piece on the intersection of AI and pure math.
→ [Read on Quanta](https://www.quantamagazine.org/archive/) (March 25, 2026)

**"The Math That Explains Why Bell Curves Are Everywhere"** — Joseph Howlett, *Quanta Magazine*
An accessible deep-dive into the central limit theorem — why normal distributions appear so ubiquitously in nature, society, and science. Great for building quantitative intuition.
→ [Read on Quanta](https://www.quantamagazine.org/archive/) (March 16, 2026)

## Science Integrity & Methodology

**"How 'Tiny Shortcuts' Are Poisoning Science"** — *Nautilus*
An investigation into how small methodological compromises — individually harmless-seeming — accumulate to undermine scientific reliability at scale. A useful lens for thinking about institutional decay more broadly.
→ [Read on Nautilus](https://nautil.us/how-tiny-shortcuts-are-poisoning-science-1279176)

## Technology & AI

**"Why Do Humanoid Robots Still Struggle With the Small Stuff?"** — John Pavlus, *Quanta Magazine*
Despite massive investment, humanoid robots still can't reliably handle everyday tasks. This piece argues the field may need to return to fundamentals rather than scaling up. A good counterpoint to hype-driven robotics coverage.
→ [Read on Quanta](https://www.quantamagazine.org/archive/) (March 13, 2026)

**"How Writing Changes Mathematical Thought"** — John Pavlus, *Quanta Magazine*
A Q&A with historian David E. Dunning about mathematical notation as a technology. The argument: the symbols we use don't just record mathematical ideas — they shape and constrain what we can think. Applies well beyond math.
→ [Read on Quanta](https://www.quantamagazine.org/archive/) (March 25, 2026)

## Biology & Natural World

**"The Jellies That Evolved a Different Way to Keep Time"** — Marlowe Starling, *Quanta Magazine*
A pea-sized jellyfish off Japan has an unusual circadian clock that works differently from nearly all other known biological clocks. Raises questions about convergent evolution and the deep history of timekeeping in life.
→ [Read on Quanta](https://www.quantamagazine.org/archive/) (March 20, 2026)

**"Why Haven't We Heard from Extraterrestrials Yet?"** — *Nautilus*
A fresh take on the Fermi Paradox, exploring the latest hypotheses for the Great Silence — from the "dark forest" theory to the possibility that intelligence is far rarer than we assume.
→ [Read on Nautilus](https://nautil.us/why-havent-we-heard-from-extraterrestrials-yet-1278966)

## Culture & Society

**"Two Is Already Too Many"** — Phoebe Arslanagic-Little, *Works in Progress* (Issue 21)
An investigation into why South Korean fertility rates have plummeted so dramatically. Goes beyond the usual explanations to examine cultural, economic, and institutional factors unique to Korea. Relevant context for the global fertility decline conversation.
→ [Read on Works in Progress](https://worksinprogress.co/)
